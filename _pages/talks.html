1. Mendelian Randomization Analysis of Multiregional Psychiatric Disorders and Their Associated Symptoms (In progress)
- Jun. 2025 – Present
- Research assistant (Supervisor: Dr. Weijie Xie), Shanghai Mental Health Center, Shanghai, China
Description:
Psychiatric disorders (e.g., depression, anxiety, addiction) are a major global health challenge. These conditions often co-occur and share biological roots, such as neurotransmitter issues and inflammation. While studies show shared genetic risks across these disorders, the direct links between genes and symptoms remain unclear.
Utilizing publicly available genome-wide association study (GWAS) data by combining Mendelian randomization (MR) methods, gene/protein analysis (TWAS/PWAS), and colocalization, we aim to develop a cross-disorder genetic causal map that highlights shared genetic influences on diverse psychopathological dimensions, and prioritize therapeutic targets with cross-diagnostic potential, grounded in causal evidence, to inform precision interventions for mental health conditions.

2. Comprehensive Single-cell Analysis of Microglia in Depression (Completed)
- Aug. 2024 – May. 2025
- Research assistant (Supervisor: Dr. Weijie Xie), Shanghai Mental Health Center, Shanghai, China
Description:
Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) is strongly linked to neuroimmune dysfunction, but the exact mechanisms of communication between microglia and GABAergic neurons remain unclear.
Using single-nucleus RNA-seq data of MDD patients and healthy controls (sourced from the GEO database), we analyzed microglial subtypes and their interactions with GABAergic neurons and prioritized key genes and biomarkers using 69 machine learning models, followed by Mendelian randomization (MR) to assess causality between microglial features and MDD risk.
Our study suggestted that impaired microglia subtypes—characterized by metabolic imbalances or chronic neuroinflammation—contribute to abnormal microglia-GABAergic neuron communication in MDD. These findings identify potential targets for restoring neuroimmune balance in MDD.

3. Immune Mechanisms of the Comorbidity of SLE and MDD (Completed)
- Jul. 2023 – Jul. 2024
- Research assistant (Supervisor: Dr. Jiaoqiong Guan), Shanghai Mental Health Center, Shanghai, China
Description:
Patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) face a higher risk of major depressive disorder (MDD), yet the shared immune mechanisms underlying comorbidity progression remain elusive.
SLE-MDD datasets from GEO underwent WGCNA and differential expression analysis to identify co-expressed modules. A panel of 101 machine learning algorithms was applied to select hub genes and construct diagnostic models, followed by immune infiltration and functional enrichment analyses. The potential biomarker was validated using public single-cell RNA-seq data, immunohistochemistry, and Western blot.
As a result, we proposed an immune target that might underlie the shared immune pathogenesis driving comorbidity progression by influencing the function and number of T-cell subtypes. Targeting this pathway may offer novel therapeutic strategies to slow the concurrent progression of both diseases.

4. Microglial Autophagy in Alleviating Depression (In progress)
- Sep. 2020 – Jul. 2023
- Master's Degree Research (Supervisor: Prof. Dr. Wuyang Wang), Xuzhou Medical University, Department of Anesthesiology, Xuzhou, Jiangsu Province, China
Description:
Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a psychiatric condition characterized by depressed mood and anhedonia, various pro-inflammatory cytokines are released from microglia, leading to neuronal excitotoxicity, synaptic pruning, and demyelination, which impact mood and cognition in individuals.
We employed various methods, including chronic restraint stress mouse models, lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced depression models (induced via lateral ventricle injection), transgenic knockout mouse models, primary microglia extraction and culture, depression-like behavioral analysis, Western blotting (WB), polymerase chain reaction (PCR), immunofluorescence staining, Golgi staining, 3D reconstruction technology, and bioinformatic analysis.
Our research focused on the molecular mechanisms of microglia in MDD and, based on this, proposed a potential targeted drug.
Microglia in the LPS group exhibited enlarged cell bodies and shortened processes, indicative of an activated state. Conversely, treatment with our drug induced microglial morphology resembling a resting state.
