1. Cell-Type-Specific Mechanisms of Schizophrenia (Completed)
- Feb. 2026 – May 2026
- Research assistant
- Principal Investigator: Assoc. Prof. Jiaoqiong Guan
- Kunming Medical University, Kunming, China
Description:
Schizophrenia is a complex neuropsychiatric disorder whose pathogenesis involves dysregulation of multiple cell types and molecular pathways. Previous genetic studies have suggested that synapse-related genes may contribute to disease development; however, the specific cell-type-specific mechanisms remain unclear.
In this project, we integrated publicly available genome-wide association study (GWAS) data with Mendelian Randomization (MR) , Transcriptome-Wide and Proteome-Wide Association Studies (TWAS/PWAS), and Bayesian Colocalization to elucidate genetically driven causal mechanisms, followed by integrative single-cell bioinformatics analyses (e.g., hdWGCNA, scFEA, scTranslator) and validation experiments using the schizophrenia mouse model.
We Identified the role of the target gene in conferring disease susceptibility within distinct neuronal populations, offering insights into cell-type-specific mechanisms underlying schizophrenia.
Integrating TWAS, PWAS, Bayesian colocalization and MR analyses
2. Cross-Disorder Genetic Architecture and Single-Cell Functional Mapping (Completed)
- Jun. 2025 – Jan. 2026
- Research assistant
- Principal Investigator: Prof. Weijie Xie
- Shanghai Mental Health Center; Shanghai Pudong New Area Mental Health Center, Tongji University School of Medicine, Shanghai, China
Description:
Psychiatric disorders including depression, anxiety, and addiction represent a major global health burden and frequently co-occur. Their comorbidity may be driven by shared biological mechanisms, for instance, neurotransmitter dysregulation and inflammatory processes. However, the shared genetic variants underlying these disorders remain unclear.
In this project, we integrated publicly available genome-wide association study (GWAS) data with Mendelian Randomization (MR) , Transcriptome-Wide and Proteome-Wide Association Studies (TWAS/PWAS), and Bayesian Colocalization to elucidate genetically driven causal mechanisms.
We constructed a cross-disorder genetic map identifying pleiotropic risk loci and prioritizing candidate genes with trans-diagnostic therapeutic potential. This approach provides causal evidence to inform interventions for psychiatric conditions.
Cross-brain-region distribution of genetic risk for psychiatric disorders
3. Microglia-GABAergic Neuron Crosstalk in Major Depressive Disorder (Completed)
- Aug. 2024 – May 2025
- Research assistant
- Principal Investigator: Prof. Weijie Xie
- Shanghai Mental Health Center; Shanghai Pudong New Area Mental Health Center, Tongji University School of Medicine, Shanghai, China
Description:
Major depressive disorder (MDD) is closely associated with neuroimmune dysfunction, though the specific mechanisms regulating microglia-GABAergic neuron communication remain unclear.
Using single-nucleus RNA sequencing data from MDD patients and healthy controls (sourced from GEO), we characterized microglial heterogeneity and cell-cell communication networks. We employed a stack of 69 machine learning models to identify and prioritize key genes, followed by MR to infer causal relationships between microglial features and MDD risk.
Our results revealed that dysregulated microglia subtypes—driven by metabolic imbalance and chronic neuroinflammation—disrupt communication with GABAergic neurons in MDD, highlighting novel potential therapeutic targets for restoring neuroimmune homeostasis in MDD.

4. Identification of an Immune Target Driving SLE-MDD Comorbidity (Completed)
- Jul. 2023 – Jul. 2024
- Research assistant
- Principal Investigator: Assoc. Prof. Jiaoqiong Guan
- Shanghai Mental Health Center, Shanghai, China
Description:
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) and Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) frequently co-occur, yet the shared immunological mechanisms underlying their comorbidity remain poorly understood. Previous studies have suggested that immune dysregulationmay contribute to both conditions; however, the specific molecular targets driving this comorbidity have not been identified.
In this project, we performed integrative bioinformatics analyses (e.g., WGCNA, machine learning) and validation experiments using the Chronic Unpredictable Mild Stress (CUMS) model, combined with molecular techniques including immunohistochemistry and Western blotting.
We identified an immune target that may underlie the shared immune pathogenesis driving the comorbidity progression of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) and Major Depressive Disorder (MDD), which holds potential for clinical translation.
5. Lysosomal Mechanisms of Microglia in Depressive-like Behaviors
- Sep. 2020 – Jul. 2023
- Master’s Student
- Supervisor: Prof. Wuyang Wang
- Xuzhou Medical University, Department of Anesthesiology, Xuzhou, Jiangsu Province, China
Description:
MDD is a complex psychiatric condition characterized by persistent low mood and anhedonia. Neuroinflammation, driven largely by microglial activation, plays a critical role in its pathogenesis, contributing to synaptic dysfunction, neuronal damage, and impaired neural connectivity.
Our lab is interested in the function of microglia lysosomes in the pathology of MDD. This project investigated the role of microglial lysosomal pathways in MDD. Experimental models included chronic restraint stress, LPS-induced neuroinflammation (via intracerebroventricular injection), and genetic knockout mice. A suite of techniques was employed: primary microglia extraction and culture, behavioral analyses, Western blot, qPCR, ELISA, immunofluorescence, Golgi-Cox staining, 3D reconstruction (Imaris), and bioinformatic analyses (R).
We identified a novel microglial lysosomal mechanism that significantly influences depressive-like behaviors, offering a potential new avenue for the treatment of MDD.
Microglia in the LPS group exhibited enlarged cell bodies and shortened processes, indicative of an activated state. Conversely, treatment with our drug induced microglial morphology resembling a resting state.
